What is RAPM?

RAPM (regularized adjusted plus-minus) measures each player's impact on point margin per 100 possessions, adjusted for everyone else on the floor. Every constant-lineup stint of every game becomes a regression row: the five players on each side are the predictors and the stint's scoring margin is the outcome. Because all ten on-court players enter each row, a player gets no credit for riding great teammates and no penalty for facing great opponents — the regression separates those effects.

The model is a ridge regression: a penalty λ (chosen by cross-validation, grouped by game so no game leaks across folds) shrinks every coefficient toward zero. Low-minute players have little evidence to push against the prior, so they land near average instead of topping the leaderboard on a lucky 30 minutes — that is the point of the regularization. Players under 200 minutes are additionally flagged and hidden by default.

ORAPM / DRAPM come from a second model with two rows per stint (one per side's offense), giving each player separate offensive and defensive impacts; RAPM = ORAPM + DRAPM. Non-D1 opponents (exhibitions, buy games) are collapsed into a single shared column so they cannot distort D1 ratings.

The small grey numbers under each rating decompose it: raw is the player's unadjusted on-court plus-minus per 100 possessions vs the D1 average; tm is the adjustment for the teammates she played with (negative when good teammates inflated her raw number); opp is the adjustment for the competition she faced (positive when a tough schedule deflated it). Raw + tm + opp ≈ RAPM — the rows don't sum exactly because ridge shrinkage and home-court are not redistributed.

Caveat: players who almost always share the floor (or almost never play apart from a star) are hard to separate statistically; ridge keeps them stable but their O/D splits deserve skepticism.

Uncertainty (the ± intervals)

Open any player to see a 95% credible interval next to her RAPM, offense, and defense (e.g. +8.2 with “95% CI ±3.1”), and a shaded band around the season-trend line. These come for free from the ridge math: ridge regression is exactly the posterior mean of a Bayesian model with a Gaussian prior on the coefficients, so each rating has a posterior standard deviation. The interval is the rating ± 1.96 standard deviations.

The interval narrows as a player logs more minutes in more varied lineups — more evidence, less uncertainty — which is why low-minute players show the widest bands. Read it as a guide to how much to trust the number: when two players' intervals overlap heavily, treat the gap between them as a tie.

These are credible intervals, not classical confidence intervals: ridge deliberately biases every estimate toward zero (the shrinkage), so the interval describes the posterior spread around a shrunken estimate rather than guaranteeing textbook coverage. They are also fairly wide and uniform — the honest signal that a single season is a limited sample. Intervals are available for the ridge model only; the lasso and elastic-net toggles have no closed-form posterior, so they show no band.

The three regression models

The leaderboard's model toggle refits the same stint design matrices with three regression families that handle the bias–variance tradeoff differently:

Ridge (the default, and what "RAPM" conventionally means) applies an L2 penalty: every player is shrunk smoothly toward zero, no one is eliminated, and collinear teammates share credit. Lasso applies an L1 penalty: it sets most players to exactly zero and keeps only those whose impact the data can clearly support — a "who actually moves the needle" view, with less shrinkage on the survivors (so their values run hotter). Elastic net blends both penalties, keeping more players than lasso but sparser and less smooth than ridge.

Season availability

Lineup-based RAPM needs substitution events, and ESPN's women's college basketball play-by-play only began recording them on February 12, 2025 (mid-2024–25). 2025–26 is the first season with full coverage and is the only season published here: 2024–25 would cover late-season games only, and no alternative source with public, full-season substitution data exists (stats.ncaa.org blocks programmatic access; NCAA.com's data API does not retain play-by-play; NCAA LiveStats / Genius Sports is credentialed-commercial). Earlier seasons have no substitution data at all. Future seasons can be added with make season YEAR=<year>.

Build

Data is pulled with wehoop (R), modeled with scikit-learn ridge regression (Python), and rendered to this fully static site. Re-run any season with make season YEAR=<year>.
